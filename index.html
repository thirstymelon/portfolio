Principles

What I believe about building systems close to hardware.
01

Registers over abstractions.

A managed runtime is a wall between you and the machine. Reading the datasheet and writing the register is slower at first — and faster forever after. I'd rather know exactly which bit I'm flipping than trust a layer I can't see through.

02

Determinate by design.

On bare silicon there's no GC, no scheduler surprise, no "it works on my machine." Behavior must be repeatable to the cycle. I design interrupt-driven, bounded, predictable systems — because on a microcontroller, a millisecond of jitter can be a missed packet.

03

Read the silicon, not the stack trace.

When something breaks at this layer, the oscilloscope and logic analyzer come before the debugger. The hardware is always telling the truth — you just have to know how to listen to it. I debug from the signal up, not the log down.

04

Portable by default.

A UART driver shouldn't be welded to one chip. I separate the hardware interface from the register layer so the same code moves between ESP32, RP2040, and STM32 with a port file, not a rewrite. Good firmware is a clean contract with the metal.
